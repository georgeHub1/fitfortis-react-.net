Most viruses are too small and cannot be photographed using visible light. Instead, they are photographed using electron beams. There are 2 types of electron microscope that are often used to record images of viruses -- SEM (Scanning Electron Microscope) and TEM (Transmission Electron Microscope).

History of electron microscopy

The first prototype electron microscope (which was a TEM), capable of 400x magnification, was developed in 1931 by the physicist Ernst Ruska and the electrical engineer Max Knoll. The apparatus was the first practical demonstration of the principles of electron microscopy. In May 1931, Reinhold Rudenberg, the scientific director of Siemens-Schuckertwerke, obtained a patent for an electron microscope. In 1932, Ernst Lubcke of Siemens & Halske built and obtained images from a prototype electron microscope, applying the concepts described in Rudenberg's patent.

In 1933, Ruska built the first electron microscope that exceeded the resolution attainable with an optical (light) microscope. Four years later, in 1937, Manfred von Ardenne created the scanning electron microscope (SEM). Siemens produced the first commercial electron microscope in 1938.

Type of electron microscopes

There are several types of electron microscopes:

  1. Transmission electron microscope (TEM)
  2. Scanning electron microscope (SEM)
  3. Reflection electron microscope (REM)
  4. Scanning transmission electron microscope (STEM)
  5. Scanning tunneling microscopy (STM)

Color

Most electron microscopes produce an image with a single brightness value per pixel, which the results usually rendered in grayscale. These images are then colorized either manually or by software to clarify structure or for aesthetic purposes. Colorizations don't add new information to the image.

Some electron microscopes can use multiple detectors, assigning different colors to secondary electron signals, which results in a color image. Electron microscopes record images of objects that are smaller than the wavelength of visible light, so strictly speaking, "color" does not have direct meaning in these images.
