Yuri's article is fairly long and complex. It's a much deeper scientific review of the claim that was previously discussed here. Below is a digest from the article, and from other sources.

The basics

The genome of SARS-CoV-2 is well understood and discussed here:

  • The viral RNA contains about 30 kilobases, encoding 29 proteins.
  • Of them, 4 are structural proteins (S - spike, E - envelope, M - membrane, and N - nucleocapsid). The other 25 are utility proteins.
  • The S protein enables the virion to attach to the victim cell, by using its ACE2 receptor.
  • The gene of the S protein comprises about 12-13% of the viral genome, and consists of 2 parts: S1 and S2. S1 interacts with the ACE2 receptor. S2 is responsible for fusion with the cell membrane and penetration into the cell.
  • The part of S1 that interacts with ACE2 is called Receptor Binding Domain (RBD). The area of direct contact is called Receptor Binding Motif (RBM).

Cast of characters

There are humans in this story...

  • Prof. Dr. Ralph Baric -- A legend in coronavirology, introducing a number of synthetic genomic manipulation techniques. Worked closely with Shi Zheng-Li on gain-of-function (GOF) research.
  • Dr. Shi Zheng-Li -- Dr Shi Zheng-Li is the leader of coronavirus research at the Wuhan Institute of Virology. Also known as "bat woman" for her research on coronaviruses in bats, she has worked under the supervision of Peter Rottier, and in collaboration with Ralph Baric.
  • Prof. Dr. Peter Rottier -- Professor at Utrecht University, who supervised the work of Dr Shi Zheng-Li on coronaviruses in the early 2000s.

... and then, there are viruses...

  • SARS-CoV-2 -- The new coronavirus, causing COVID-19.
  • RaTG13 -- A coronavirus found in bats, which is the obvious ancestor of SARS-CoV-2. Its genome is 96% equivalent to that of SARS-CoV-2.
  • MP789 -- A coronavirus found in 29 pangolins, which were confiscated by customs from smugglers in the Guangdong province. All 29 pangolins experienced death in captivity, due to the virus, despite the efforts of the rescue workers.

Sequence of events

First let's look at the sequence of events:

When What happened
February, 2000 The Dutch group of Peter Rottier from Utrecht University publishes a paper demonstrating creation of a coronavirus that can cross host cell species barrier, by substitution of parts of its S protein.
2002 Ralph Baric and his group publish a breakthrough paper, demonstrating the first synthetic assembly of a natural murune coronavirus.
November 16, 2002 SARS-CoV epidemic outbreak identified in Foshan, Guangdong, China.
October 28, 2003 Ralph Baric and his group publish a paper on synthetically recreating SARS-CoV. What is notable here is how quickly a qualified team of virologists can re-create a virus synthetically.
2005 Shi Zheng-Li, Peter Rottier and others publish a paper discussing what allows viruses to expand their host range.
January 2007 Ralph Baric and his group publish a paper describing a lab-modified human SARS-CoV, improved in mice, which turned to be a highly virulent and lethal virus.
2007 Shi Zheng-Li's group at the Wuhan Institute of Virology (WIV) publishes a study of the S protein of human and bat coronaviruses.
2013 Shi Zheng-Li's team isolates RaTG13 from bats in a Yunnan cave. However, outside of her lab, no one knew about that strain until January 2020.
October 30, 2013 Shi Zheng-Li's group publishes a paper in Nature announcing that in Yunnan caves they had discovered carrier bats of the RsSHC014 and Rs3367 strains that coincided with the first SARS-CoV by 85% and 96%, respectively. Shi Zheng-Li gets the moniker "bat woman".
2015 Shi Zheng-Li and Ralph Baric publish a joint paper in Nature, which becomes the most famous gain-of-function (GOF) virology paper.
February 18, 2016 Shi Zheng-Li publishes a paper on RaBtCoV/4991 - a new coronavirus that's practically identical to MP789.
March 15, 2016 The group of Ralph Baric in collaboration with Shi Zheng-Li publishes a paper on insertion of a S protein segment into a mouse-adapted SARS.
November 30, 2017 Shi Zheng-Li's group publishes a paper on the creation of 8 new viruses, transplanting RBDs from bat SARS-like viruses, collected from the cave in Yunnan.
2017 Another batch of pangolins get confiscated, with a similar virus (Pangolin P2V/2017).
March-December 2019 4 Chinese pangolins (Manis pentadactyla) and 25 Malayan pangolins (Manis javanica) are confiscated by the Customs and Department of Forestry of Guangdong Province from smugglers. These animals were sent to the wildlife rescue center in Guangdong, and were mostly inactive and sobbing, and eventually died in custody of severe coronavirus symptoms, despite exhausting rescue efforts. Tissue samples were taken from the lung, lymph nodes, liver, spleen, muscle, kidney, and other tissues from pangolins that had just died for histopathological and virological examinations. The coronavirus from the pangolins (MP789) gets isolated.
October 2019 Chinese scientists in Beijing demonstrate insertion of furin into a live chicken coronavirus - infectious bronchitis virus. As a side note the addition of a furin site allows the mutant virus to infect nerve cells (loss of smell, anyone?)
October 2019 The group of Ralph Baric submits a paper on the importance of S protein protease cleavage (see Furin below) to crossing the barrier to zoonotic infection by coronaviruses.
January 10, 2020 The SARS-CoV-2 genome is sequenced and made publicly available.
January 23, 2020 Shi Zheng-Li releases a paper indicating that CoV2 is 96% identical to RaTG13, a strain which her laboratory had previously isolated from Yunnan bats in 2013. The paper gets published in Nature too.
February 13, 2020 A team in Hong Kong releases a paper that notes clear signs of recombination in the S protein of SARS-CoV-2.
February 17, 2020 Chinese scientists discover a peculiar strain of pangolin coronavirus in their possession, which, while generally being only 90% similar to CoV2, in the RBM region was almost identical to it, with only a single amino acid difference.
February 21, 2020 Swiss scientists publish a paper, demonstrating creation of a synthetic clone of SARS-CoV-2.

The smoking gun...

Now, let's look at genome similarities. This first chart shows that the genome of the bat coronavirus RaTG13 (in blue) has a very high level of identity to the genome of SARS-CoV-2. The original 2003 SARS-CoV virus (in red) is quite different genetically.

There's a notable dip in similarity around the S protein encoding, and more specifically in the area of the RBM. At the same time, the pangolin coronavirus strain (2019-nCoV-WIV02 / MP789) has a RBM that almost perfectly matches what we see in SARS-CoV-2:

The red line above is the line of genome similarity of the pangolin coronavirus (2019-nCoV-WIV02 / MP789) and SARS-CoV-2.

In other words, there must have been some strange interaction between bats (from Yunnan) and pangolins (from Guangdong), with exchange of bodily fluids, even though they are naturally separated by more than 1100 miles (!!!).

Furin

Last but not least - the furin cleavage site. Here is the detailed diagrams of the S protein gene. One of the key additions in the SARS-CoV-2 genome is the furin site between S1 and S2. Furin is the most effective protease -- an enzyme that cuts RNA, thus increasing greatly the virality of the virus, as demonstrated in this study.

Topological diagram of the S protein gene.

The encoding for furin is just 12 bases long (ccucggcgggca). It has been recently demonstrated that the absence of furin greatly decreases the pathogenicity and replication ability of the virus.

Here's a visual representation of the phylogenetic tree of coronavirus genomes. It is interesting that SARS-CoV-2 is the only virus from its closest family, with furin. The closest relative with furin is BAT-CoV-HKU5, isolated in 2014 by Shi Zheng-Li's team. It's a very distant relative though -- the S proteins of SARS-CoV-2 and BAT-CoV-HKU5 share only 36%.

How hard is recombination?

Genetic recombination is the exchange of genetic material. It occurs naturally. It can also be done artificially by using enzymes. These days, artificial recombination is fairly easy. There are many companies that are selling recombination kits, like this one.

How fast is mutation?

Natural (in vivo) mutations that would lead to the 4% difference in the genomes of SARS-CoV-2 and RaTG13 would take years. However, lab (in vitro) mutations can be performed much faster, as the passage can be sped up. A paper from 2004 reported achieving a 2.1% difference in genomic sequences of spike protein, after only 600 passages.

Putting it all together

So we have a situation where the new Coronavirus (SARS-CoV-2) is very similar (96%) to another virus (RaTG13) isolated from bats in 2013 (but unknown to the world until January 2020) EXCEPT in the RBM region of its spike protein. The RBM region of SARS-CoV-2 almost perfectly matches (with the exception of a single amino acid) what we see in a third virus (2019-nCoV-WIV02 / MP789), which otherwise is quite different from SARS-CoV-2.

There's a notable insertion of furin - which makes the virus a lot more viral.

And, to top it all off, the 2 ancestor viruses (the "bat virus" and the "pangolin virus") were found in 2 very different species (bats and pangolins) more than 1100 miles away from each other. Note that bats are not sold in the Wuhan wet market where the outbreak originated.

All of this clearly indicates the strong possibility of human-driven recombination -- the act of combining different parts of different RNAs in the same RNA strand. Combine that with a steady history of lab leaks (H1N1 in 1977; SARS-CoV in summer of 2003, in December 2003, and spring of 2004; etc. - lab leaks are unfortunately quite common) and you might just get yourself a nice global pandemic...
