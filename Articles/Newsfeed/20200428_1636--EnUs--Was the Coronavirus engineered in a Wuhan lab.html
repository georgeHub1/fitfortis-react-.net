Was the new Coronavirus "engineered" in a Wuhan lab, by using artificial selection techniques? A Newsweek article suggests that may indeed be the case.

What you should know

  • In March 2020, US intelligence agencies acknowledged the possibility that SARS-CoV-2, the Coronavirus that causes COVID-19, originated in a Chinese lab. Previously, it was believed that the virus originated naturally and spred from the wet market in Wuhan.
  • So far, the virus has infected at least 3.1M and has killed more than 210,000 people worldwide. The US alone has ~60K fatalities.
  • The Wuhan Institute of Virology (WIV) has collected more bat coronaviruses than any other virology lab in the world. WIV has also conducted experiments to engineer viruses artificially. The institute has published only 4 scientific publications on the topic of viral research in 2020.
  • In 2018, the institute built a BSL-4 lab, enabling transmissibility (serial passage) experiments. In these experiments, the virus is injected into one animal. When the animal gets sick, viral particles from its body are injected into another animal. This process is repeated until an animal catches the disease only by exposure to another sick animal.
  • The animals used in the transmissibility experiments are typically ferrets, considered to be good stand-in for humans.
  • Transmissibility experiments in animals are controversial, because they result in the creation of dangerous viruses through artificial selection. Selection is an indirect genetic engineering technique, so mutations in the virus are completely consistent with natural evolution.
  • US diplomatic communication from 2018 shows that the US embassy staff were worried about the experiments conducted at the lab for fear that a virus could escape the lab and cause a pandemic. According to Josh Rogin in the Washington Post, the two diplomatic cables that were dispatched to Washington "warned about safety and management weaknesses at the WIV lab".

Biosafety level 4 (BSL-4) labs

Research on dangerious biological agents is performed in special enclosed laboratory facilities, differentiated by their levels of containment. The levels of containment range from the lowest biosafety level 1 (BSL-1) to the highest at level 4 (BSL-4). These levels are specified by the CDC in the United States, and by a directive in the EU.

Biosafety level 4 (BSL-4) is the highest level of biosafety precautions, and is appropriate for work with agents that could easily be aerosol-transmitted within the laboratory and cause severe to fatal disease in humans for which there are no available vaccines or treatments.
